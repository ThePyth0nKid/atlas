# Crit: compliance on Atlas V2 Vision

> **Reviewer:** compliance / regulatory specialist agent (Phase 2 Doc A + Doc C focus, with Doc B/D/E cross-references).
> **Reviewer stance:** Atlas's product narrative is heavily EU-AI-Act-centric. My job: verify legal claims are accurate, surface jurisdictional/sectoral gaps, flag where engineering promises exceed what the law actually says, and clearly distinguish "team can self-determine" from "external legal counsel mandatory."
> **Source-of-truth notes:** Where I quote an Article, I cite the verbatim Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 text or the proposal text as last amended. Where I cannot, I flag **"legal counsel required"** explicitly. None of this critique is itself legal advice.

## Stärken

1. **The Article 12 anchor is structurally sound.** Doc A §3.1 + Doc C R-S-02 + `docs/COMPLIANCE-MAPPING.md` (V1.7) already do the verbatim-quote-and-mechanism-mapping work. Article 12 §1 of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 actually reads *"High-risk AI systems shall technically allow for the automatic recording of events ('logs') over the lifetime of the system"*, and §2 requires logging *"appropriate to the intended purpose of the system"* with the explicit goal of facilitating post-market monitoring and ensuring traceability. Atlas's signed, hash-chained, Rekor-anchored events.jsonl + offline-WASM-verifier is a strong factual fit for the *technical means* of satisfying this — better than most vendor-controlled logging solutions because the *"independently verifiable"* property is structural, not contractual. The in-force date (2 August 2026 for high-risk systems under Art. 113(b)) is correct.

2. **GDPR conflict identified, not ignored.** R-L-01 + Doc B §3.3 directly name the Art. 17 vs. immutability tension and propose content-hash separation with a tombstone event type. This is the only architecturally honest answer and it is identified pre-implementation rather than post-incident. Most "blockchain for healthcare" projects elide this question entirely; Atlas does not.

3. **License-trap risk surfaced for the graph DB.** R-L-02 names FalkorDB's SSPLv1 status before any V2-α code is written, identifies Kuzu as a documented MIT alternative, and proposes abstracting over the graph layer. This is mature legal hygiene — most projects discover SSPL exposure during a fundraising due diligence, not during architecture review.

4. **Witness federation respects regulator-side operational reality.** Doc B §2.10 (lifecycle steps 1-4) correctly insists the regulator generates the witness keypair on a regulator-controlled HSM, with the public key handed out-of-band — i.e., the regulator never trusts Atlas with their private key. This mirrors how existing regulatory PKI works (e.g., national qualified-trust-service-provider key ceremonies under eIDAS) and is the only architecturally defensible model.

5. **Marketing language already moderated in the right direction.** R-S-02's recommendation to say *"designed to satisfy the verifiability and tamper-evidence requirements of EU AI Act Art. 12"* rather than *"EU AI Act compliant"* is the correct framing. Under Art. 43 + Annex VI/VII, "compliance" is a conformity-assessment determination that only a notified body or self-assessing provider can make about a specific high-risk system — not something a substrate vendor can claim. The risk matrix flags this correctly.

6. **V1's COMPLIANCE-MAPPING.md is unusually rigorous.** The existing V1.7 mapping already provides verbatim Article text + Atlas mechanism + inspectable-test pairs across EU AI Act Art. 12 / Annex IV §1(e) / Art. 14, GAMP 5 Appendix D11 (ALCOA+), and ICH E6(R3). This is materially above the industry baseline; V2 should *extend* it, not replace it.

## Probleme (by severity)

### CRITICAL

**C-1: "Independently verifiable" is Atlas's phrasing, not the Regulation's.** Doc A §3.1 + the V1.7 mapping use *"independently verifiable"* as the operative phrase for Article 12. The verbatim Regulation 2024/1689 Article 12 text does *not* contain the words *"independently verifiable"*. It requires *"automatic recording of events ('logs')"* with a level of traceability *"appropriate to the intended purpose"*, plus (in conjunction with Annex IV §2(g)) the ability of deployers and notified bodies to *interpret outputs*. The closest the Regulation gets is Annex IV §1(g) ("validation and testing procedures used") and the post-market monitoring obligations in Art. 72. **Risk:** if Atlas writes marketing copy or compliance whitepapers asserting "Article 12 mandates independently verifiable event logs," a sophisticated competitor or regulator can fact-check this and conclude Atlas is overselling. The substance of Atlas's architecture is genuinely better than what the Regulation requires — but the marketing should reflect what the law actually says. **Action:** legal counsel required to re-state §3.1 mapping using verbatim Article 12 + Annex IV §1(g) + Annex IV §2(g) language, then add the design-claim *"Atlas provides cryptographic independent-verifiability that exceeds the Article 12 minimum."* Self-fix attempt without counsel is not safe given the consequence of being publicly corrected.

**C-2: GDPR Art. 4(1) "personal data" interpretation of hashes is the highest-stakes open legal question and is currently single-sourced.** Doc B §3.3 asserts *"hash exists, anchor exists, signature exists → 'this event existed at time T'"* preserves auditability while permitting GDPR Art. 17 erasure of underlying content. R-L-01 Q2 already flags that "whether a hash of deleted content is personal data" is a legal question. **The conservative reading is unfavourable to Atlas:** under CJEU C-582/14 *Breyer* (2016) the test is whether re-identification is "reasonably likely," and EDPB Guidelines 4/2019 + Article 29 WP Opinion 4/2007 treat hashed identifiers as pseudonymous (still personal data) unless the hash is built with a cryptographically-strong salt that the controller does not retain. A blake3 hash of arbitrary payload bytes with no salt is *trivially* reversible if the plaintext space is small (names, emails, structured records). **Implication:** Atlas's content-hash-separation may *not* satisfy Art. 17 under the strict reading favoured by German BfDI and several EU DPAs. The architectural mitigation requires either (a) per-content random salt destroyed at deletion time + hash-of-salted-content stored (which then becomes uncorrelatable post-deletion), or (b) a tombstone-replaces-hash pattern that re-anchors the chain without the original hash — which breaks V1's immutability invariant. **Action:** **legal counsel required, pre-V2-α blocking gate.** Specifically: written opinion from a German/Irish/French data-protection-specialised firm (those three because Germany is strictest, Ireland is most-litigated, France has recent CNIL precedent on hashing). This is the single most important pre-launch legal question.

**C-3: Witness-federation legal model has no documented EU regulatory precedent.** Doc B §2.10 + Doc A §4.1 + Doc E Demo 2 all present "regulator runs a witness key, cosignatures land in real time" as a novel structural capability. **It is novel, but the "novelty" is also its risk.** There is no existing EU AI Act, GDPR, DORA, or sectoral regulation that *names* this pattern or grants it special status. The closest existing analogues are:
  - **BaFin §44 KWG on-site banking supervision** — supervisor has on-site access but does not co-sign each event;
  - **GDPR Art. 27 EU representative** — local point of contact, not a cryptographic primitive;
  - **eIDAS qualified trust service providers (Regulation 910/2014)** — supervisor maintains trusted lists (TSLs) but does not run a runtime witness;
  - **EBA Guidelines on outsourcing arrangements EBA/GL/2019/02 §83-91** — supervisor "access and audit rights" but again not a cryptographic primitive;
  - **FDA 21 CFR Part 11 §11.10(e)** — audit trails that are *secure, computer-generated, time-stamped* — closest in spirit, but FDA does not run keys.

**No EU regulator (BaFin, FCA-UK, FINMA, AMF, BaFin, AFM, AustraDA, BaFin) has publicly endorsed or piloted "supervisor cosignature" as a recognised compliance pattern.** This means Demo 2's "BaFin-witness-eu" is not just illustrative — it is *aspirational* in a way that may attract critical pushback from the very compliance officers it targets. **Action:** legal counsel required to confirm Atlas's marketing language for this feature ("regulator-friendly architecture," not "regulator-approved pattern"); plus one-page legal brief on "what existing supervisory pattern is closest" — that's the credible anchor point. The current "BaFin's witness key is illustrative" disclaimer in Doc E Demo 2 is necessary but insufficient; the *positive* framing also needs to be downgraded.

### HIGH

**H-1: Article 13 mapping is too narrow.** Doc A §3.1 maps Art. 13 (Transparency to Deployer) to *"V2 Read-API lets Deployer query 'what did this agent do for this user in this session'"*. Verbatim Art. 13 §1: *"High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed in such a way to ensure that their operation is sufficiently transparent to enable deployers to interpret a system's output and use it appropriately."* §2 + §3 then enumerate specific information items including: characteristics of the system, performance metrics, level of accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity referred to in Art. 15, foreseeable circumstances of misuse, the input data specifications, and the human oversight measures of Art. 14. **Atlas's Read-API addresses only the runtime-behaviour subset.** The full Art. 13 obligation is on the *provider* of the high-risk system and includes *static* information that Atlas does not provide (training data characteristics, accuracy metrics, intended purpose statement). Implication: Atlas can claim to *contribute to* Art. 13 transparency, not *deliver* Art. 13 transparency. **Action:** team can self-fix the mapping language; no counsel needed.

**H-2: Article 14 mapping conflates "design" obligation with "evidence" capability.** Doc A §3.1 maps Art. 14 (Human Oversight) to *"Cedar policy enforcement at write-time: policies REQUIRE human-cosign for sensitive event-types — pre-action enforcement, not just post-hoc audit."* Verbatim Art. 14 §1: *"High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed […] in such a way […] that they can be effectively overseen by natural persons."* §3 enumerates oversight measures including (a) provider-built measures, (b) deployer-implemented measures, (c) interpretation/intervention/override capability. **Cedar enforcement is a strong fit for §3(c) but does not by itself satisfy §3(a)+(b).** A regulator could read Atlas's "Cedar at write-time" as compatible with Art. 14 but not as sufficient for Art. 14. **Action:** team can self-fix mapping; align language to *"Atlas provides the technical substrate for §3(c) intervention/override; provider remains responsible for §3(a)+(b) human-oversight design."*

**H-3: Article 18 retention mapping has a verbatim error.** Doc A §3.1 states *"Art. 18 (Record retention, 6 months minimum)"*. **The verbatim Article 18 §1 says: "Providers […] shall keep the documentation referred to in Article 11, technical documentation referred to in Annex IV, the EU declaration of conformity referred to in Article 47, decisions made by notified bodies, *for a period ending 10 years after the high-risk AI system has been placed on the market or put into service*."** Article 19 §1 then separately states *"the logs referred to in Article 12 shall be kept for a period of at least 6 months."* Atlas's docs collapse Art. 18 (10-year doc retention) and Art. 19 §1 (6-month log retention) into one. **Action:** team can self-fix; this is a typo-class error, but visible in any compliance review. The substantive question is whether Atlas's log retention should default to 6 months (Art. 19 minimum), or the deployer-configurable retention covering both (some sectoral regs — pharma GMP, banking BCBS 239, MiFID II — require 5-7 years). Recommend: Atlas defaults to 7 years retention to satisfy the longest mainstream sectoral requirement; deployer can configure shorter for non-sectoral-regulated workspaces.

**H-4: Articles 9, 15, 17, 26, 50, 55 are absent from the Atlas mapping.** Doc A §3.1's table maps only Art. 12/13/14/18. The Regulation has many more high-risk-provider obligations Atlas substrate is *directly relevant to*:
  - **Art. 9 — Risk management system.** Atlas events.jsonl can serve as evidence of risk-management decision records.
  - **Art. 15 — Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity.** Atlas's cryptographic integrity *directly* satisfies the cybersecurity sub-paragraph (§5: *"resilient as regards […] attempts by unauthorised third parties to alter their use, outputs or performance"* — tamper-evident logs are textbook evidence).
  - **Art. 17 — Quality management system (QMS).** Atlas events.jsonl + GAMP 5 ALCOA+ mapping already in V1.7 docs is QMS-evidence material.
  - **Art. 26 — Obligations of deployers of high-risk AI systems.** §1: ensure use according to instructions; §5-6: monitor operation, retain logs (cross-ref Art. 19); §12: human oversight under Art. 14. Atlas is highly relevant to §5-6.
  - **Art. 50 — Transparency obligations for general-purpose AI (GPAI) providers + AI-generated-content disclosure.** §2: machine-readable mark on synthetic content. Atlas events.jsonl with `author_did = agent:*` is exactly this kind of provenance signal.
  - **Art. 55 — Obligations for GPAI models with systemic risk** (>10²⁵ FLOPs threshold per Art. 51): model evaluation, adversarial testing, serious-incident reporting, cybersecurity. Atlas is substrate-relevant for the incident-reporting and cybersecurity dimensions.
  
  **Action:** team can self-draft an expanded Article-mapping table (no counsel needed for the expansion itself), but submit final version for counsel review. This is a *defensive-moat* item: every additional Article Atlas can credibly map widens the addressable-compliance-narrative.

**H-5: EU AI Liability Directive status claim is now stale and possibly wrong.** Doc A §3.2 says *"EU AI Liability Directive (proposed, expected 2026 enactment)"*. **The European Commission withdrew the AI Liability Directive proposal in February 2025** (Commission Work Programme 2025, COM(2025) 45 final, item under "Withdrawn proposals," cited justification: lack of foreseeable agreement). The Council had been blocked since 2023. Doc A's "Council-Phase 2025/2026" framing is factually outdated. **Implication:** Atlas's §4.2 ("AI-Liability-Insurance pricing substrate") narrative depending on the Directive's enactment must be rewritten. The *existing* fallback regime is the revised **Product Liability Directive (Directive (EU) 2024/2853, entered into force 2024-12-08)**, which explicitly covers software including AI systems and shifts the disclosure burden when claimant cannot prove defect. PLD is in force, AILD is not. **Action:** **legal counsel required** — confirm PLD's actual coverage of AI memory/audit-log scenarios and whether Atlas's evidence-trail value-prop is reframable from "AILD-driven" to "PLD-driven." This is at least a session of compliance-counsel work to redraft §3.2 and §4.2; not a self-fix.

**H-6: AI-BOM regulatory backing is over-claimed.** Doc A §4.5 + Doc D §5.4 present AI-BOM as an emerging compliance artifact Atlas can substrate-for. **No current regulation mandates AI-BOM.** CycloneDX 1.5 ML-BOM and SPDX 3.0 AI Profile exist as drafts/standards but are not legally binding. The US Executive Order 14028 SBOM mandate was rolled back under the new administration; CISA's SBOM-for-AI work continues but is non-binding guidance. EU NIS2 references SBOM in Annex II §3 but does not mandate AI-BOM specifically. The strongest current driver is *contractual* (large enterprise procurement contracts increasingly require AI-BOM); not regulatory. **Action:** team can self-fix; reframe AI-BOM as *"emerging procurement-driven artifact, not yet a regulatory mandate; Atlas's events.jsonl structure positions us to be substrate-ready when standardisation lands."*

**H-7: Insurance regulatory framing is missing.** Doc A §4.2 makes the AI-Liability-Insurance-pricing-substrate claim but has no engagement with how insurance regulation actually works in the EU. Insurers in the EU operate under **Solvency II (Directive 2009/138/EC, as amended)** which mandates own-risk-and-solvency assessment (ORSA), Pillar 1 capital requirements, and Pillar 3 disclosures. AI-specific underwriting in Europe is *also* constrained by **EIOPA's June 2024 Opinion on AI governance and risk management in the (re)insurance sector** which lists six principles including documentation/recordkeeping. An insurer adopting Atlas-attested events as a pricing input must demonstrate ORSA-conformity of that input. **Implication:** the "Munich Re / Hartford / Swiss Re" narrative needs an insurance-regulation-aware counterparty conversation, not just a sales conversation. **Action:** legal counsel required for the insurance-regulation-mapping section; or alternatively, postpone §4.2 to V2-γ until counsel work is funded.

## Blinde Flecken

1. **No AI Office / national-competent-authority engagement plan.** Atlas's regulatory-fit story depends on at least one supervisory authority either endorsing or piloting the witness-federation pattern. The current docs propose marketing to compliance officers at *deployers*, not to *supervisors*. The reverse motion (AI Office consultation submission, BaFin AI Office contact, Dutch AP innovation hub, French CNIL "bac à sable" sandbox) would change Atlas's posture from "vendor claiming compliance fit" to "substrate participating in regulatory conversation." None of the five Phase-1 docs name this.

2. **No reference to EU AI Office Implementing Acts.** Article 12 §3 (logging level appropriate to risk class) and Annex IV technical-documentation specifics are subject to implementing acts to be adopted by the EU AI Office. The Office's first set of guidance documents and CoP (Code of Practice) for GPAI was finalised mid-2025; further sectoral guidance is rolling out 2026-2027. Atlas's mapping must track these documents — they will operationally redefine what "Art. 12 compliant" means before the team finishes V2-α. No tracking mechanism exists in the current docs.

3. **No DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) framework.** Any Atlas deployment touching personal data triggers DPIA obligations under GDPR Art. 35 §1. For high-risk AI Atlas deployments, the Art. 35 DPIA combines with the AI Act Art. 27 Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA) — the FRIA was finalised in the 2024-08-01 final text and is now mandatory for deployers of high-risk AI in public-services contexts. Atlas should publish a *DPIA-and-FRIA template* customers can adapt. This is high-ROI marketing collateral that costs ~1 week of counsel + drafting.

4. **No qualified-trust-service-provider (QTSP) angle under eIDAS.** Sigstore Rekor anchoring satisfies "advanced electronic timestamps" but not "qualified" under eIDAS (Regulation 910/2014 + Regulation 2024/1183 eIDAS 2.0). For some regulated workflows (notarial, judicial-evidence, certain insurance claims), only qualified timestamps from a QTSP have the legal presumption of validity. Atlas could (a) explicitly say "we are not eIDAS-qualified, here's why that's not the threshold the EU AI Act actually requires," or (b) explore a partnership with a QTSP to offer qualified-timestamp mode for highest-stakes deployments. Either is a legitimate position; neither is currently in the docs.

5. **No cross-border data transfer / Schrems II framework.** Atlas's hosted-sync (Doc A §6.4 Personal/Team/Enterprise tiers) and the multi-witness federation (Doc B §2.10) imply trans-Atlantic and intra-EU data flows. GDPR Chapter V (Arts. 44-50) imposes adequacy/SCC/BCR requirements; the EU-US Data Privacy Framework (in force 2023-07) provides current adequacy but is under active legal challenge (Schrems III is pending at the CJEU, expected decision 2026-2027). Atlas's enterprise tier needs an SCC + DPA template. None of the five docs touches this.

6. **No conformity-assessment-self-claim risk analysis.** Atlas providing a substrate that "enables Art. 12 compliance" is fine. But customers integrating Atlas into a high-risk AI system must perform their own conformity assessment (Art. 43, Annex VI or VII). If Atlas's marketing leads a customer to skip or under-perform their conformity assessment, Atlas could face derivative liability under the revised PLD 2024/2853 (which expressly covers digital products that contribute to a defect). The product disclaimers and customer-facing documentation must be explicit: *"Atlas is a substrate, not a conformity-assessment tool; integrating Atlas does not substitute for your Art. 43 obligations."*

7. **No serious-incident-reporting (Art. 73) flow.** Article 73 requires providers of high-risk AI to report serious incidents to the market surveillance authority within 15 days (immediate notification within 2 days for breach of obligations relating to high-risk system + serious incident; 10 days for malfunction-only). Atlas events.jsonl is *exactly* the substrate for evidence-gathering during a reportable incident. A "serious-incident-report builder" CLI/UI is high-value collateral that is not in any doc.

8. **No Annex VIII registration entry coverage.** Art. 49 requires providers and deployers to register high-risk AI systems in the EU database referred to in Art. 71. The registration data is enumerated in Annex VIII. Atlas's event-types include some Annex-VIII-relevant items (intended purpose statements, foreseeable misuse). A "registration-data export from Atlas" pattern is missed surface.

9. **No discussion of "AI literacy" obligation under Art. 4.** From 2 February 2025, providers and deployers must ensure sufficient AI literacy of staff dealing with AI systems. This is a *training* obligation; Atlas signed events.jsonl can prove "operator X completed AI literacy module Y on date Z, signed by training-provider key K" — Atlas-as-AI-literacy-attestation-substrate. Missed angle.

10. **No reference to FINMA, CSSF, AFM, AMF, BaFin AI-supervision pilots.** Several EU/EEA financial supervisors have AI-supervision pilots running 2025-2026 (FINMA AI workstream, CSSF Circular 22/806 on AI risk management, BaFin AI principles paper). Each is a potential anchor reference for the regulator-witness federation positioning. None of the docs name these.

## End of Crit

**Reviewer's net assessment:** Atlas's structural compliance-fit is genuinely strong — better than most "AI compliance" vendors — and the team has been disciplined enough to flag the largest risks (R-L-01 GDPR, R-L-02 SSPL, R-S-02 regulatory-interpretation-lock-in) before V2-α. The Phase-1 docs already do above-baseline work. But the *marketing-language precision*, *jurisdictional breadth*, and *counsel-engagement plan* are not yet at the level required for a credible EU-AI-Act-narrative-driven V2 GTM. Three of this critique's items (C-1 verbatim-language fix, C-2 GDPR hash-classification, H-5 AILD-withdrawal) are *visible* in any sophisticated competitor's or counsel's review — they need to be fixed before V2-α public materials are written, not after. The good news: the substance of Atlas's architecture is genuinely defensible; the language and scope of the compliance claims need tightening, not the underlying engineering.
