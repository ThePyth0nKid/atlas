# Crit: product on Atlas V2 Vision

> **Author:** Product / UX critique agent (Phase 2). **Scope:** Doc A (Strategic Positioning) + Doc E (Demo Sketches), with persona/journey/conversion lens applied across Docs B/C/D where they shape user-experience surface area. **Stance:** "Would the user understand and care?" + "Would the demo convert a viewer to a try-it-yourself click?" Atlas's strongest weakness is that *cryptographic provenance* sounds like infrastructure plumbing to most humans; this critique is built around that pressure point.

---

## Stärken

- **Doc A §4 — "New Trust-Modes" framing is the single most differentiated section.** §4.1 (Continuous Regulator Attestation), §4.2 (Insurance Pricing Substrate), §4.3 (Agent Passports), §4.4 (Pre-Action Cedar Enforcement), §4.5 (AI-BOM), §4.6 (Cross-Org Federation) are genuinely novel product narratives, not commoditisable compliance plumbing. The "Insurance discount" framing in §4.2 in particular is the rare line that translates "Ed25519+Rekor+Witness" into "−30% on your premium" — that's a UX-grade message for a CFO who never opens DevTools.
- **Doc A §1.3 candidate 2 — "Knowledge your AI can prove, not just claim"** is the strongest tagline in the set because it works in plain English without naming any cryptographic primitive. It tracks with the README's existing strapline ("knowledge the agent can prove, not just claim"), so Atlas already has internal consistency on this exact phrasing — a good sign that the verbal hook is robust enough to survive Phase 3.
- **Doc A §2.3 — "engineering economy of scale" framing is correct.** A single substrate, two GTM narratives, two UI surfaces is a much cleaner mental model than two products. The fact that `human:nelson@ultranova.io` vs `agent:hermes-instance-xyz` is just a string in the identity field is a structurally honest pitch — it makes the two-market story defensible without forking the engineering tree.
- **Doc A §7 R-P-4 ("Cryptographic Complexity as Adoption Bremse") and Doc C R-O-02 ("UX Complexity Barrier") both name the right risk.** "Hide the machinery; surface only Verified ✓ / Tampered ✗" with the HTTPS-lock-icon analogy is the correct UX strategy, and it's named explicitly in both documents (Doc A §7 / Doc C R-O-02 mitigation). The risk is acknowledged; what's missing is a concrete UX vocabulary spec — see Probleme.
- **Doc E §Methodology — the 5-block storyboard (Setup / Action / Reveal / Implication / CTA) is a usable production template.** It maps cleanly onto modern landing-page demo conventions and forces narrative discipline. The honesty constraint ("if the demo requires a capability Atlas doesn't have yet, the readiness flag must say so") is a refreshing piece of internal product discipline — most pitch materials fudge this.
- **Doc E Demo 1 (Multi-Agent Race) — visible-in-10s differentiator is the right hero hypothesis.** Two color-coded agents writing into one shared verifiable graph is the rare demo where the unique structural property is visible without narration, captions, or a developer audience. The recommendation in Doc E §Demo Selection (Demo 1 = hero, Demo 2 = enterprise CTA secondary) is sound under the criteria given.
- **Doc E Demo 4 (Verifiable Second Brain) Vignette B — the "Obsidian misses tampering" moment is the strongest viscera-moment in the entire demo set.** A red banner appearing on the Atlas pane while the Obsidian pane silently accepts a forged paragraph is the single sequence in this whole document where a non-technical viewer feels something (mild dread + then relief). That emotional design instinct is exactly right — even if the production blockers are heavy (see CRITICAL).
- **Doc E Demo 5 Reveal — "Cache for speed. Verifier for trust. Never the same thing."** is a single line that packages the three-layer architecture (authoritative log + queryable graph + fast cache) into a memorable phrase. It is a tag the engineer-evaluator audience will *repeat to their colleagues* — that's product-marketing gold.
- **Doc C R-O-02 mitigation language is unusually concrete** for a risk doc: "two states visible to non-technical users: Verified (green checkmark) and Tampered (red X). All cryptographic detail one click away." That's almost a design spec; product can pick it up and ship it.
- **Doc A §3.3 ("Was V2 *nicht* tut") and Doc E's per-demo "Honest weakness" / "Risks" sections show willingness to bound the scope.** Saying out loud what Atlas is *not* is a product-management discipline that protects the messaging from drift.

## Probleme (by severity)

### CRITICAL

- **The "Verify" button moment is treated as the climax of every demo, but the docs never define what the click feels like from a non-technical user's perspective.** Doc E Demos 1, 2, 3, and 4 all end the Reveal block with "click Verify → modal opens → ✓ VERIFIED appears." For an AI engineer, that's satisfying. For a Compliance Officer or an Obsidian power user, it raises a question Atlas does not answer: *"what was I supposed to feel before I clicked, and what changed after?"* The "Verified" state has no meaning until the user already trusts the verification *paradigm*. The HTTPS-lock-icon analogy in Doc A §7 R-P-4 is the right reference but it omits the crucial detail: HTTPS works because the **absence** of the lock is a visible loss of state in the browser chrome. Atlas demos show only the positive case (✓ VERIFIED). There is no equivalent visible-in-chrome failure state. This is the **single biggest UX hole** in the demo programme — the verifier-click is treated as a payoff but is structurally a no-op for any user who isn't already cryptographically-literate.
- **Doc A §2 two-market positioning is structurally coherent but operationally undefended.** §2.3 argues "same substrate, two narratives" but the docs never articulate the decision rule for *which market drives each piece of communication*. Doc A §7 R-P-1 says "marketing discipline: each communication chooses ONE primary persona" but that's a constraint, not a method. In practice, two-market positioning fails not because the substrate is wrong but because the landing page, the docs nav, the README, the demo loop, and the pricing page all need to make a *single* "first impression" choice — and there is currently no source of truth saying which market is the front door. **Without that decision in Doc A, every downstream surface will silently pick its own primary, and Atlas will read as "fancy compliance plumbing" to AI engineers and "weird crypto thing" to compliance buyers.** Doc D §10 raises the same concern from a market-segmentation angle ("does the positioning need to be sharpened to lead with regulated-vertical buyers?") — converging answers needed in Phase 3.
- **Doc E Demo 4 (Verifiable Second Brain) is described in detail as if it were filmable, but the product surface it depicts does not exist.** Doc E §Demo 4 readiness flag is honest about this ("V2-β + V2-γ + new UX" and "this is the demo we cannot fake"), but the demo's body reads as a finished product walkthrough. The risk is not the storyboard itself — it's that **if this demo lives in Phase 1 docs as a v0, it becomes a phantom commitment.** Phase-3 synthesis will read "we have a Second Brain demo planned" and forget the readiness flag. Recommend either (a) cutting Demo 4 from v0 entirely and replacing it with a "future-state aspirational" sidebar (clearly non-shippable), or (b) downgrading it to a 1-paragraph concept sketch — not a full storyboard — until V2-γ exists. Currently it sits at the same fidelity level as Demo 1 (the hero), which over-weights the Second-Brain market in every reader's mental model.
- **No demo shows a failure mode.** All five demos terminate in ✓ VERIFIED or 🟢 cosigned. There is no demo that says: *"the witness was offline for 3 hours — here's what the user sees."* Or: *"a projection drift was detected — here's the recovery."* Or: *"a tampered note was found in a non-paranoid Atlas vault — here's the diff."* (Demo 4 Vignette B is the only failure-mode demo and it's the most-blocked demo in the set.) **Trust products live or die on how they fail, not how they succeed.** Doc C R-A-01 (Projection Determinism Drift), R-A-03 (Agent Key Compromise + Revocation Lag), and Doc C Q6 ("rebuild vs tamper UX distinction") all surface this as a substantive product question. A "Failure-Mode Demo" — even just 30s of "the regulator's witness key was rotated; here's how a verifier handles it" — is a credibility multiplier that no competitor (Anthropic Memory, Mem0, Letta) could match.

### HIGH

- **Doc A §1.3 tagline candidates 1, 4, 5, 6 are all variants of "trust me, this is verifiable."** They lead with the differentiator instead of leading with the user's pain. A test: rewrite each tagline as the user's complaint, then check whether the tagline is a *response*. "TÜV-Plakette für AI agent memory" (candidate 4) is a response to "I don't know which agent told me what"; "Where AI agents write knowledge that holds up in court" (candidate 6) is a response to "if this goes wrong, who is liable." Candidate 2 ("Knowledge your AI can prove, not just claim") is the only one that does this naturally because "claim" implies the existing untrustworthy state. Recommendation: lead with candidate 2 for the AI-engineer surface, candidate 6 (or a rewrite of it) for the compliance surface, and quietly drop candidates 1/3/4/5 from Phase 3.
- **Doc A §2.1 Second-Brain personas (4 listed) are not validated.** They are written in the present tense as if they exist: "Researcher mit AI-Assist", "Knowledge-Team in regulated Field", "Indie Maker / Solopreneur", "Privacy-conscious Power-User." Doc A §8 Q-1 honestly acknowledges this ("Ist 'Verifiable Second Brain' ein echter Markt oder eine aspirational category?"), but the persona section reads as if the answer were already yes. **No user research, no interview record, no Discord conversation, no Twitter poll is cited.** For a Phase-2 product critique this is the single largest evidence gap: the four personas should be downgraded to "hypotheses to validate via N=5 interviews per persona" before any landing-page copy targets them.
- **Doc A §2.2 AI Engineer persona is more credible but still untested.** "AI Engineer / Agent Builder" who wants to know "this fact has author X, written at time T, signed" is a plausible buyer — but is the *desire* for cryptographic attribution today, or is it *educational work Atlas has to do first*? Mem0's hyper-growth (52K stars, AWS Strands SDK) suggests AI engineers prioritize retrieval primitives over trust primitives. No interview record cited here either. Recommendation: P0 user-test = 5 Hermes-Agent users + 5 LangGraph/LangChain users asked "would you trade 1s of additional latency for cryptographic attribution of every memory write?" Until that answer is known, the AI-Engineer Primary-Market hypothesis is unvalidated.
- **Doc E §Demo 1 framing ("race") is acknowledged as risky in Doc E itself but the alternative is not specified.** Doc E says "the 'race' framing could feel gimmicky — alternative framing: 'two specialists collaborating', 'agent + human reviewer'." This is the right instinct but the alternative is half a sentence. **For the landing-page hero, the framing decision is more consequential than the engineering scope.** A "race" demo to engineers might land as "cute"; a "collaboration" demo to a CCO might land as "trust by design." Recommend Phase 3 lock the framing before any production work starts. My recommendation: drop "race," use "two specialists, one verifiable workspace" — keeps the visible-in-10s color-coding but loses the gimmick taste.
- **No demo shows the install-and-first-success path.** Doc E Open Q13 acknowledges this ("we are NOT covering the developer setup experience"). For the AI-engineer audience — the explicit V2-α/β/γ target — the *fastest* differentiation Atlas owns is "npm install @atlas-trust/verify-wasm + 50 LOC of glue = your agent has cryptographic memory." A 30-second TUI demo of `npm install + node verify.js → ✓ VALID` is arguably the highest-converting demo Atlas could ship today, and it requires zero V2-α/β work. Currently it doesn't exist. Recommend adding a Demo 6: "30-Second Install + First Verify" with readiness flag TODAY.
- **Doc E Demo 2 production-complexity is rated 3/5 but the hardest part — getting an actual regulator to operate a witness — is rated as a sales/BD activity rather than a product blocker.** The "BaFin-witness-eu" disclaimer ("illustrative — see disclaimer") is honest but creates a credibility problem: if the demo's protagonist (the regulator's key in the system) is *fictional*, the entire emotional payoff is undercut for any compliance buyer who recognizes BaFin doesn't actually run the witness. **Demo 2 will not close any enterprise deal until one real regulator (or one named insurance underwriter, or one Big-4 audit firm) has committed to running a witness node.** Doc E Open Q4 + Q12 raise this but it should be CRITICAL for the compliance GTM, not a "TBD demo design question."
- **The two-market story collapses into one demo programme that primarily serves Market A (AI Engineer).** Demos 1, 3, 5 = AI Engineer. Demo 2 = Compliance. Demo 4 = Second Brain (and is unshippable). The actual ratio of demo-attention to market-importance is 3 : 1 : 1 (with Demo 4 effectively at 0 because it requires unbuilt UX). If Doc A §2 is right that the two markets are co-equal, the demo programme should reflect that — or Doc A §2 needs to admit AI Engineer is primary and Second Brain is secondary (a defensible position; see Open Question for Phase 3).
- **"Hide trust by default" is named as a UX principle (Doc A §7 R-P-4 + Doc C R-O-02) but Doc E demos systematically violate it.** Every demo's Reveal block surfaces "Ed25519," "Rekor logIndex," "witness cosignatures," "Sigstore Rekor anchor." Demo 2's right-side log is literally `T+0.0s — Event 4,892 received. Ed25519 signature OK. Hash chain append confirmed. Parent ref c9f...` — that is *the opposite* of HTTPS-lock-icon UX. A consistent rule should be: **the cryptographic terms appear only inside a click-to-expand DevTools-style panel, never in the demo's first-pass visible frame.** Demos 1, 2, 3 need to be re-edited to apply this rule before they are filmed.

### MEDIUM

- **Demo 3 (Agent Passport) opens with vocabulary the market does not have yet.** "Hire verified AI agents. Bring their track record with them." is good copy for someone who already accepts that agents *have* track records. For a 2026 viewer the leap is enormous: most viewers have never thought of an AI agent as having a CV. The demo asks a viewer to accept three premises (agents have identities, identities accumulate history, history is portable across organizations) before the first verifier-click. **Demo 3 may need to come AFTER Demo 1 in the demo programme even though Doc E Open Q7 suggests it might be the conceptual prerequisite.** Concrete recommendation: lead with Demo 1 (concrete: I see two agents writing), then Demo 3 (concept: agents have keys, keys have history) — the order matches how a viewer's mental model is actually built.
- **Doc A §1.3 Tagline 3 ("Cryptographic Second Brain for the agent era — trust is structural, not transactional") buries the verb.** "Trust is structural, not transactional" is a wonderful phrase for an internal team but a viewer needs a verb early. A user reads the tagline asking "what does this do for me?" — "Cryptographic Second Brain" is a thing, not an action. Compare to "Notion is the connected workspace" or "Obsidian is the place where your knowledge thrives." Atlas's verb is "prove" or "verify" — Tagline 2 already has the verb; Taglines 1/3/4/5 do not.
- **Doc A §6.5 GTM sequencing recommendation is correct but the cashflow argument is unaddressed.** §6.1 (Hermes Open-Core) + §6.4 (Open-Core Pricing) as Q1-Q3 + §6.2 (Enterprise) as Q4+ assumes the open-core early phase produces enough cash to fund the enterprise sales cycle. With Atlas at zero paying users today and a 6-12 month enterprise sales cycle in §6.2, the cashflow plan is implicitly "Nelson personally funds the runway." That may be the answer but Phase 3 should make it explicit instead of letting it sit unsaid in §6.5.
- **Doc E §Demo Selection for Landing Page Hero recommends Demo 1 but doesn't discuss platform-fit per demo (Open Q15).** A 45-second autoplay-mute loop is the right hero spec for atlas-trust.dev, but the same Demo 1 will fail on X/Twitter (needs to be 15s, sound off, attention-grab in first 2 seconds), and the enterprise sales-deck version of Demo 2 needs to be 3 minutes with narrator. **Master one + trim is the wrong answer if the platform mechanics differ this much.** Recommend Phase 3 ship a per-platform spec: hero 45s autoplay, X 12s, LinkedIn 30s, YouTube 90s, sales deck 180s. Each is a different *edit* of the same source material.
- **Demo 5 (Mem0g) is defensive (Doc E Open Q11 names this).** It addresses "isn't crypto slow?" which implicitly concedes the worry exists. For the AI-engineer audience who will run the Mem0g benchmark in their head, the demo is necessary. For the landing-page first-pass viewer it's not — the speed objection isn't even on their mind yet. Recommend keeping Demo 5 in the docs/architecture page (where it answers a viewer's already-formed question) and NOT in the landing-page demo carousel.
- **Doc A §5 Obsidian comparison ("Atlas bietet die Obsidian-Mechanik (local-first + paid sync) PLUS cryptographic trust PLUS multi-agent-write") significantly overstates Atlas's current readiness for that market.** Obsidian's actual strengths are: (a) 2,750+ plugin ecosystem, (b) markdown-on-disk portability that Obsidian users can survive Obsidian-the-company disappearing, (c) 10-year mature mobile + desktop experience, (d) graph view + canvas + Bases. Atlas has zero of those today. Saying "atlas bietet die Obsidian-Mechanik PLUS X" is technically true *for the local-first-paid-sync revenue mechanic alone*, but the comparison reads as if Atlas competes with Obsidian on UX — it doesn't. Recommend Phase 3 sharpen this to: "Atlas adopts Obsidian's pricing mechanic (free local + paid hosted) but is not a markdown editor — it's a verifiable substrate that an Obsidian plugin or competing PKM frontend can sit on top of." Doc D §3.1 already says this honestly ("Building 'Atlas-as-Obsidian-plugin' might be the cheapest, fastest distribution route") — Doc A §5 should match that honesty.
- **Doc E Open Q9 ("how honest should we be about V2 readiness in the demos themselves?") needs a concrete answer not a question.** A reasonable default: every demo carries a small bottom-right corner badge — `TODAY` (green), `V2-α/β COMING` (amber), `ASPIRATIONAL` (grey). Conversion-rate impact is small; credibility-with-engineers impact is large. Phase 3 lock this.
- **No demo shows what onboarding feels like.** Doc E Open Q13 names it as a gap. The 30-second-quickstart UX — clone repo, sign first event, see ✓ VERIFIED — is the single most powerful "this is real" moment for a curious developer. It's also the shortest demo in the catalog (no Cytoscape, no animation, no Hermes-purple branding). Recommend ship a Demo 6 (Quickstart) that is 30 seconds, TUI-only, real Atlas writes — readiness flag TODAY.
- **Demo 1's CTA ("npm install @atlas-trust/verify-wasm — atlas-trust.dev") does not match the demo's audience emotion.** The demo's audience emotion is "trust + power" — they just watched two agents prove themselves to each other. The CTA "npm install" is functional but emotionally flat. Better CTA: "Watch your agents prove themselves — atlas-trust.dev/start" with the npm install as the first line of the docs page. Same conversion mechanic, better emotional bridge.

That's a real psychological moment and it triggers the user's existing-but-not-articulated fear about AI memory. Phase 3 consider.

## Blinde Flecken

- **No discussion of the install-and-first-success funnel.** The single conversion event Atlas controls today is the npm install. Doc A §6 GTM Hypotheses discuss adoption mechanics at the market level but never specify: what happens between "user lands on atlas-trust.dev" and "user signs their first event"? How many clicks? How many commands? What's the dropoff? This is the most measurable, most-improvable, most-undiscussed part of the entire product surface. The atlas-trust.dev landing page in Doc E is a hero-video page, not a quickstart funnel.
- **No persona has been interviewed.** Doc A §2.1 lists four Second-Brain personas + four AI-Engineer personas (§2.2). None of them is cited as derived from actual conversation with a human matching that description. This is the single largest evidence gap in the entire Phase 1 corpus. Doc A §8 Q-1 acknowledges it for Second Brain specifically; the AI-Engineer side is not even challenged.
- **The Hermes Agent skill UX is undefined.** Doc A §6.1 names "Atlas Memory Skill for Hermes" as the first distribution vehicle. Doc B §2.x mentions atlas-mcp-server expansion. Neither document describes what installing the skill *looks like* for a Hermes user: what command, what config, what success state, what failure state. For the most-strategic distribution channel in Atlas's GTM, the first-touch UX is unspecified. Phase 2 question: is the install-success path *the* most-important V2-α deliverable? Probably yes.
- **Failure-mode UX is undesigned.** When verification fails — bad signature, missing anchor, expired witness key, projection drift — what does the user see? Doc C R-A-01 (Projection Drift) and Q6 ("rebuild vs tamper distinction") name this as an open question but no demo, no storyboard, no UX spec addresses it. **Trust products are defined by their failure surface; Atlas has no failure surface designed.**
- **Onboarding for non-technical users is unspecified.** Doc A §2.1 targets researchers, knowledge teams, indie makers — all non-technical-or-light-technical. A non-technical user installing Atlas needs (a) zero command-line, (b) workspace setup wizard, (c) key-management that doesn't require understanding what a key is, (d) sync that "just works." None of this is in any doc. The Personal Tier in Doc A §6.4 ("€5-10/mo") assumes this UX exists; it doesn't.
- **CTA architecture is missing.** Doc E lists per-demo CTAs ("npm install", "Schedule a compliance briefing", "Get the Atlas Second Brain beta"). For the landing-page programme these CTAs need to map to (a) sign-up funnel (email capture, no payment), (b) GitHub star (community signal), (c) Discord join (engagement loop), (d) free trial (paid funnel), (e) enterprise contact form (sales pipeline). Currently every demo's CTA is a different kind — no coherent funnel architecture exists.
- **Trust narrative complexity for the 60-second CCO pitch is unwritten.** Doc A §3.2 (AI Liability Directive) is a strong adult-conversation framing for a CCO who already understands EU AI Act. But the 60-second elevator pitch — "you are a Chief Compliance Officer at a bank, I have one minute, here's why Atlas matters" — does not exist anywhere in Phase 1. Phase 3 should ship: 60s pitch per primary persona (AI Engineer / CCO / Knowledge Worker). Each pitch <120 words.
- **Multi-language UX surface is undiscussed.** Atlas's strongest DACH-compliance audience reads documentation in German; the AI-engineer audience reads in English; the Anthropic/OpenAI/Vercel-style design language is English-by-default. Does atlas-trust.dev ship i18n from day one or English-only? This is a product decision with marketing consequences and it sits nowhere in Phase 1.
- **Mobile is unaddressed.** All five demos in Doc E describe desktop scenes. Atlas Personal Tier (Doc A §6.4) and Demo 4 Vignette A explicitly mention mobile sync — but the mobile UX, mobile signing UX, mobile verifier UX are unspecified. Mobile is where most Second-Brain users live (Capacities is "mobile-first"; Obsidian Mobile is mature). Atlas Personal Tier cannot ship without mobile.
- **The "scary screenshot moment" is missing.** Trust products convert through emotional shock — Snowden + "your phone is listening" + "your password leaked." Atlas has the right ingredients (Obsidian-doesn't-detect-tampering in Demo 4) but no demo or doc *isolates* that moment for social media. A 6-second GIF of "Obsidian: nothing. Atlas: red banner." is the most-shareable artifact Atlas could ship — and it doesn't exist in the Phase 1 plan.
- **No Discord / community plan.** Atlas needs a community to support adoption (Obsidian's Discord has 100K+ members; Mem0's GitHub Discussions is active). Doc A §6 mentions "Reddit / Hacker News / r/LocalLLaMA presence" once and never names a Discord, Slack, or forum. For an open-core product this is the bottom of the funnel where most retention happens.
- **A11y / WCAG is unmentioned.** Atlas plans to sell to EU-regulated enterprises (Doc A §6.2). EU directive 2019/882 (European Accessibility Act) is in force June 2025. atlas-web shipping without WCAG 2.1 AA is a procurement blocker for several of Atlas's target verticals. Doc A doesn't surface this.
- **Brand identity is "TODO" in the README itself (line 1-7) — Doc E Cross-cutting §1 acknowledges this.** But a brand-less product launching a landing-page hero demo is incoherent. Phase 3 needs a brand-kit gate before any demo production starts.

## Konkrete Vorschläge

- **Doc A §1.3 — Phase 3 should lock tagline candidate 2 ("Knowledge your AI can prove, not just claim") as the universal Atlas tagline.** It works for both markets (substitute "your AI" → "your team" for the Second-Brain segment). Drop candidates 1, 3, 4, 5 (or relegate to internal use). Keep candidate 6 ("...holds up in court") as a *secondary* tagline reserved for AI-Liability-vertical materials only. This collapses six options to two and removes a decision-debt landmine.
- **Doc A §2.3 — Add a one-paragraph "Primary Market Decision Rule":** "When Atlas communicates to a single audience (landing page hero, conference talk, demo programme), the primary market is **AI Engineer / Agent Builder**. Second Brain / Knowledge Worker is the secondary market, served via crossover narratives (researcher-with-AI-assist) but never as the front door." This makes Doc A §2 operationally usable for every downstream surface decision. (Open for Phase 3 to flip the primary if the cashflow argument in §6 demands enterprise-first.)
- **Doc A §4 — Split into two subsections: §4-near-term (V2-α/β shippable: §4.1 Regulator Witness, §4.3 Agent Passports, §4.4 Cedar Enforcement) and §4-future-state (§4.2 Insurance Pricing, §4.5 AI-BOM, §4.6 Cross-Org Federation).** This protects the demo programme from over-committing to 2027+ stories.
- **Doc A §5 — Rewrite the Obsidian comparison to remove "Atlas bietet die Obsidian-Mechanik PLUS X" and replace with "Atlas is not an Obsidian competitor — it's a verifiable substrate that an Obsidian plugin (or competing PKM frontend) can sit on top of."** Match Doc D §3.1 honesty.
- **Doc A §7 — Add R-P-8 ("Failure-mode UX undesigned") as a new HIGH-severity positioning risk.** Trust products are defined by how they fail; Atlas has no failure surface designed. Mitigation: Phase 4 ship a failure-mode UX spec before V2-α.
- **Doc A §8 — Add Q-17 ("What is the install-and-first-success funnel and what is its conversion rate target?")** as a Phase 3 question. This is the single most-improvable, most-measurable surface Atlas owns today.
- **Doc A §8 — Add Q-18 ("Have we interviewed any persona from §2.1 or §2.2?")** as a P0 evidence question. Phase 2.5 should run N=5 interviews per primary persona before Phase 3 locks the GTM sequencing in §6.5.
- **Doc E Demo 1 — Drop the word "race" from the framing. Use "Two specialists, one verifiable workspace."** Keeps the visible-in-10s color-coding (the actual differentiator); loses the gimmick taste; works in compliance and engineering audiences without translation.
- **Doc E Demo 1 — Apply the "Hide trust by default" rule to the Reveal block.** The side panel should NOT lead with "did:atlas:hermes-a-9f7c3..." and "Rekor logIndex 2,718,281." It should lead with "Signed by Hermes-instance-A ✓ and Claude-via-MCP ✓ — both verified" with a small `[ Inspect crypto details ]` link to expand. The technical detail belongs in a click-to-expand DevTools-style panel.
- **Doc E Demo 2 — Replace the fictional "BaFin-witness-eu" placeholder with a clearly-labeled "Regulator-Witness (illustrative)" framing AND make securing one real witness operator (regulator, insurer, audit firm) a pre-launch GTM blocker in Doc A §6.2.** Demo 2 is the enterprise-deal-closer in Doc E — it should not be filmed with fictional protagonists.
- **Doc E Demo 4 — Downgrade from a full storyboard to a 1-paragraph "future-state aspirational" sidebar.** Mark it as V2-γ-or-later.
